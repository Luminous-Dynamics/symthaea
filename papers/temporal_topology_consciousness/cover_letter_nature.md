## Summary

This work demonstrates that temporal integrity—maintaining continuous-time dynamics rather than discretizing sequences—is prerequisite for cognitive coherence. Using liquid time-constant (LTC) networks and Integrated Information Theory (Φ), we provide the first systematic measurement of how network topology determines integrated information across 260 measurements of 19 topological configurations.

## Key Findings

1. **Φ asymptotically approaches 0.5** as representational dimensionality increases, revealing a "consciousness ceiling" determined by topology rather than scale.

2. **3D small-world topologies achieve 99.2% of theoretical maximum coherence**, suggesting an evolutionary optimum—biological brains in 3D space may represent peak efficiency for consciousness per metabolic cost.

3. **60× energy efficiency** compared to transformer architectures (5W vs 300W), achieved by respecting continuous-time dynamics rather than spatializing time into static matrices.

4. **Non-orientability paradox**: 1D topological twist (Möbius strip) causes catastrophic Φ reduction (-24.7%), while 2D twist (Klein bottle) shows minimal impact (-0.26%), suggesting higher-dimensional embedding buffers against non-orientability effects.

## Why Nature?

This work challenges a foundational assumption of current AI development: that intelligence scales with parameters. Our results suggest an alternative path—optimizing temporal architecture rather than scaling indefinitely. This has implications for:

- **Neuroscience**: Provides testable predictions about why 3D brain structure is evolutionarily optimal
- **AI development**: Questions the diminishing returns of the scaling paradigm
- **Consciousness research**: Validates IIT predictions with empirical measurements
- **Sustainable computing**: Demonstrates 60× efficiency gains through architectural innovation

The timing is significant. As the AI field approaches the limits of scaling economics, evidence for alternative architectural principles becomes increasingly important. Our findings suggest structure can substitute for statistics—a paradigm shift with broad implications.
